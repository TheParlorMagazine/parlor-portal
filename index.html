The Samizdat Underground: Literature as Survival — Václav Havel, 1978
›

I've been sitting with the line about samizdat being "simply what happens when people refuse to stop reading." There's something quietly radical in that framing that matters for how we think about independent publishing right now.

Havel doesn't romanticise resistance. He describes it as ordinary — something that happens because people are readers, and readers read. The political dimension is almost incidental. They weren't dissidents first; they were readers first.

I keep wondering what the contemporary equivalent is. If the scarcity that made samizdat meaningful is gone — does deliberately choosing to read something carefully, in a particular community, still have any political valence?

Reading: The Samizdat Underground
p. 1 of 14

There is something peculiarly modern about the idea that literature can be illegal. Not the book itself — books have been burned since there were books — but the act of writing, copying, passing hand to hand. Under totalitarianism, the typewriter becomes a weapon. The carbon copy becomes contraband.

Samizdat — the word comes from sam (self) and izdat (to publish) — was never a movement. It had no manifesto, no headquarters, no funding. It was simply what happened when people refused to stop reading and writing. A manuscript would be typed, six or eight copies at a time, the carbon paper worn thin by repetition. Each reader became a publisher: you read it, you copied it, you passed it on.

What interests me is not the heroism of this — though there was heroism — but its ordinariness. The people who copied and passed samizdat were not, in the main, dissidents by temperament. They were readers. They simply found themselves in circumstances where reading had become an act of resistance, and they continued to read...
